Penn set to observe the Palestra's 90th birthday

By Brevin Fleischer

The Palestra, opened in 1927, will celebrate its 90th anniversary this January. Often called the “Cathedral of College Basketball,” the Palestra is the most storied gymnasium in the country, having hosted more games since its inception than any other arena.

In addition to being a famed home to college basketball, the Palestra hosts Penn volleyball, gymnastics and wrestling. The arena not only serves as one of the most prominent landmarks on Penn’s historic campus, but it is also a treasure to the city of Philadelphia as a whole.

Enshrined on a plaque in the arena is its motto: “To win the game is great. To play the game is greater. But to love the game is the greatest of all.” These words are reflected throughout the Palestra’s hallowed halls and are echoed by all who have had the honor of coaching and playing at the nation’s finest arena.

Jay Wright, the newly minted national champion coach at Villanova and frequent visitor to the Palestra said in a 2014 Washington Post story, “One of my great disappointments in life was realizing I wasn’t good enough to play in the Big 5. To be able to come back as a coach and be part of it has been an amazing part of my life.”

With a maximum capacity of 8,700 people, the Palestra’s atmosphere is intense and enthusiastic with each seat seemingly right on top of the action. Filling those seats are throngs of students eager to absorb Big 5 action at the pseudo-conference’s official home court.

Not just home to the Big 5 however, the Palestra’s court has also been graced by basketball legends from Wilt Chamberlain to Kobe Bryant to Lebron James. The Palestra’s walls are lined with images capturing the gym’s greatest games, most legendary shots, and most famous players, littering in stars like Chamberlain among local Philadelphia legends.

No stranger to national attention, the Palestra was the subject of a 2007 ESPN Classic broadcast that documented the gym’s history, using the Palestra’ story as a microcosm for the history of college basketball as a whole. Additionally, ESPN lead college basketball analyst Jay Bilas referred to the Palestra in 2005 as “one of the true gems of the sports world, right up there with Wrigley Field.”

Jay Wright, agreeing with Bilas’s sentiments, said about the Palestra, “There really isn’t anything like it anywhere else in basketball.”

This 90th anniversary season is a great opportunity for Penn students to reflect Wright’s sentiments and to exemplify their love for one of the country’s most famous sports venues. Penn students must acknowledge and celebrate how fortunate they are to have total access to a building as storied as the Palestra, because Penn truly is unique amongst its Ivy League rivals with its deep entrenchment into the very fabric of sports history. We can no longer take this fact for granted. Let’s fill those 8,700 seats this season.
