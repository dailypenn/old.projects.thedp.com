Offense Outlook

By Andrew Zheng

Too many scorers. It’s every coach’s dream, but for the Quakers, it might be a reality.

One of the hallmarks of last year’s Ivy title-winning side was that on any given day, there was a different player stepping up and taking charge of the Red and Blue offense.

That seems unlikely to change this year.

With the team’s top six scorers all returning for either their junior or senior seasons, there is little reason to believe that the Quakers will have any difficulty repeating the offensive success of last season.

Offensive success for Penn, however, does not simply mean running up the scoreboard or having the league’s leading scorer. It means finding what’s working on a weekly basis, knowing that the winning formula will change many times over the course of a season.

All of the Red and Blue’s top six scorers had at least one game with the team’s highest point tally.

“I think what we learned last year — and what we are learning this year because we are a completely different team — was knowing who was on,” junior guard Lauren Whitlatch said. “Everyone is going to be on in a different game.”

For all of the team’s depth, there are still some constants that persist from game to game. Down low sit the team’s two arguably most potent offensive threats, junior forward Michelle Nwokedi and senior forward Sydney Stipanovich, who led the team in points last season with 407 and 371 respectively.

Coach Mike McLaughlin is open about the fact that his team was set up through the two post players, something that shows no sign of changing in this new campaign. But what really gives the Quakers’ offense that special edge is seeing how the backcourt fits into the equation.

“I think it’s going to be really hard for teams to guard us because we have the two best posts in the league,” junior guard Anna Ross said. “But even when they aren’t at their best, they can pass it out and we’ll hit threes, or we’ll find another way of scoring.”

“Obviously their main focus is typically Michelle and Sydney down low,” sophomore guard Ashley Russell added. “So if we move when they get the ball to the corner we can be open.”

McLaughlin expects a lot out of his guards, and the players are well aware of that. Ross, Russell and Whitlatch see themselves as having a myriad of responsibilities: ranging from moving the ball around to sinking open shots to calming teammates down when things might go wrong.

“Last year we had situations where Michelle and Sydney didn’t score and the guards did,” McLaughlin said. “We had it vice versa. I think that’s a good formula for overall to have a good season, and we’re hoping to continue on that path.”

At the end of the day, what McLaughlin strives for is both a selfless and independent style of play, one that promotes a team culture but also lends agency for each player to make her own decisions.

“I want to show them how to play and then have them play free and relaxed,” the eighth-year coach said. “I think if we can teach them how to do certain things and empower them to go play the game freely, I think that we’ve done our job.”

Whereas some coaches might be concerned with their stars not getting all the touches they might need to stay in rhythm from game to game, McLaughlin brushes it aside, citing that having one of the league’s leading scorers does not build a championship winning side.

The players have bought in too.

“I think we’re all in,” Whitlatch said. “We play as a team, we don’t play as individuals”
